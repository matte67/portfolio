## The problem

General property portals optimise for price and location, but student housing also depends on trust, routines, shared-space expectations, and compatibility. A technically successful search can still produce a poor living experience.

UniStays reframes the unit of discovery around the room and its human context. Students can inspect an apartment, available rooms, current flatmates, and location before starting a conversation.

## Role and contribution

The project moved from idea to a functioning product with Matteo responsible for UI/UX, the JavaScript frontend and backend work, external integrations, analytics, real-time chat, and listing management.

## Product architecture

The interface separates three concerns: discovery for students, inventory management for hosts, and verification or moderation for administrators. Firebase supports application data and real-time communication, Clerk handles identity, Google Maps anchors geographic search, and GA4 supports product analytics.

### Important decisions

- Treat the room—not only the apartment—as a first-class listing entity.
- Keep map and result filters synchronised so geography remains visible during refinement.
- Attach conversations to listing context rather than offering an unstructured inbox.
- Publish flatmate information only with consent and pair reporting with moderation workflows.

## Challenges and trade-offs

Trust features add friction to listing publication, but reduce uncertainty for students. Compatibility data can improve decisions, while requiring careful consent and privacy boundaries. A map-led interface improves spatial understanding, but must remain usable on narrow screens and without precise location permissions.

## Outcome

The result is an independently designed and developed private beta spanning search, filters, maps, favourites, chat, host listing management, moderation, and analytics. The current Netlify deployment is a preview rather than the final public release. The product demonstrates breadth, but still needs real-world validation before claims about matching quality or marketplace scale can be made.

## Lessons learned

Product architecture is shaped by the social model as much as the technology. Treating rooms, residents, consent, and conversations as explicit concepts produces clearer boundaries than adapting a generic property schema after the fact.
